There's nothing like a good night's sleep to help you feel refreshed. But sleep provides much more than a chance to rest your body.

Findings from a new study suggest that getting a good night's sleep also protects your brain. The effects of not getting enough sleep are jarring: Getting fewer than five hours of sleep per night doubles a person's risk of developing dementia. Inadequate sleep increases a person's risk of dying from premature causes, too.

In this short video, I'll describe the details and findings of the study. I'll also talk about some of the mechanisms that drive the beneficial effects of sleep on the brain.

One of these mechanisms occurs primarily during slow-wave, or deep, sleep. During deep sleep, the brain activates the glymphatic system, a series of cerebrospinal fluid-filled vessels that surround the brain and comprise the brainâs very own self-cleaning scheme. Toxic waste products and other cellular debris are pumped out of the vessels during sleep to preserve brain health.

One of these toxic products is amyloid-beta, a protein that clumps together and forms plaques in the brain as we age. Amyloid-beta plaques are a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease. Poor sleep promotes the build-up of amyloid-beta, driving a vicious cycle of poor sleep and amyloid-beta accumulation.
