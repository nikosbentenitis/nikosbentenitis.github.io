Coolest Things I Learned This Week

​
​Write Like a Painter

Writing gets easier when you think of yourself as a painter, where each draft is a sketch that builds up to the final painting at the end.

People freak out about first drafts because they write them as if others will see them. Artists take a different approach. Knowing that nobody will see their sketches silences the eye of judgment so they can play with ideas.

These sketches from Edward Hopper reveal a principle for the creative process: play first, perfect later.

Why Writing is Harder Than It Looks

Readers think that ideas take a clear and obvious path, while writers know all the dead ends they explored to reach their final destination. The simpler an idea looks, the crazier the journey probably was.

This reminds me of two of my favorite paradoxes, which I distilled in this thread:​

  1. The paradox of writing: Great writing looks effortless. But because the ideas are so clear, casual readers don't appreciate how much time it took to refine them.
    ​
  2. The paradox of creativity: Your work is done when it looks so simple that the consumer thinks they could've done it, which means they won't appreciate how hard you worked.

History Class

When you walk around New York, it becomes obvious that there should be classes where kids explore the city instead of sitting in class all day.

With all the museums and historical sights, it seems criminal to put kids in tiny classrooms all day. Take an art history class. Why read about Emanuel Leutze when you can visit the MET and see his George Washington's crossing of the Delaware River painting for yourself?

Yes, there are safety concerns but students in cities don’t spend nearly enough time learning outside the classroom.

Tests Should Be Open Book

Speaking of alternatives to education, I can’t believe that we have so many closed-book tests. Most of life is an open book test, and school should reflect that.

Sure, there are times where memory is important. But we spend too much time memorizing things in school. As a writer, I outsource the storage of details to my note-taking system. I think in hyperlinks. Instead of memorizing details, I memorize where those details are stored in my Second Brain.

This feels like a digitally native way of thinking, and I’d like to see more tests reflect that.

Genetic Fundamentalism

People are increasingly debating the morality of genetically modified babies. In the future, there will be a group called "Genetic Fundamentalists."

They will fight for babies to be 100% made by Mother Nature. Those who argue for designer babies will say that "Genetic Fundamentalists" are unethical because they're not giving their children every possible advantage.

Thanks to Ellen Fishbein for introducing this idea to me.

Photo of the Week

I was so excited to be back in New York that I walked 32,000 steps on Saturday.

I stopped by Rockefeller Center on Saturday night to see the architecture. Like much of New York, it was designed in the 1920s. Since architecture is how we express the psychology of a culture, it reflects that time’s ambition. Through technological grandeur, the buildings always inject me with a dose of towering optimism.

For example, the mural above is a celebration of knowledge. In the middle, you’ll see the word “Thought.” On its side, “written word” and “spoken word.” Then, each figure around it is a celebration of a different discipline like poetry and philosophy. Moving through the space, I was reminded that the world is a better place when we transcend minimalism and celebrate the pursuit of knowledge.
