In April, data belonging to over half a billion Facebook members was leaked. This data breach, which affected users from the US, UK, India, and over 100 other countries, highlights an important issue: social media networks pose a threat to our privacy.

Facebook Hack: A Quick Recap

In early April, a hacker shared personal data — including full names, phone numbers, email addresses, and more —  from 533 million Facebook users on a hacking forum. About 32 million were linked to US accounts. Ironically, Mark Zuckerberg’s phone number was included in the leak, as well. 

The breach appears to relate to an old Facebook vulnerability that the social media network reportedly fixed in 2019 — but not before cybercriminals stole hundreds of millions of user account details. 

Important: If you haven’t yet checked whether your data was compromised, you can do so via The News Each Day or Have I Been Pwned.

Not the First Time

Facebook has a long history of failing to protect user privacy. Here’s a non-exhaustive timeline:

2007: Facebook came under fire for Beacon, a part of Facebook’s advertisement system that tracked user purchases and shared this information with users’ friends, often without their consent.

2011: The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) accused Facebook of deceiving users into thinking that their information on Facebook was private when in fact, it was public and shared with advertisers.

2013: In June, a bug exposed the private contact information of 6 million Facebook users. In September, it came to light that the National Security Agency (NSA) used public Facebook profiles to map people’s social connections. 

2014: Facebook once again made headlines, this time for allowing scientists to manipulate Facebook users’ emotions without their knowledge.

2018: In March, The Guardian and The New York Times reported on the Facebook-Cambridge Analytica data scandal, which concerned a voter-profiling company accessing millions of Facebook users’ personal data without their consent. In September, a security breach affected millions of Facebook users. In December, a bug exposed users’ unposted Facebook photos to third-party apps.

2019: In February, The Wall Street Journal reported that third-party apps are sharing highly sensitive information, like users’ heart rates and menstrual cycles, with Facebook. In March, leaked internal Facebook documents revealed that the company was mounting a worldwide effort to lobby against data privacy laws. Also in March, Facebook faced a criminal probe over data sharing with other tech firms. In April, millions of Facebook user records were exposed on Amazon Cloud Servers. That same month, Facebook unintentionally uploaded email contacts of 1.5 million users without their consent.

2020: The cybersecurity intelligence firm Cyble found a database of a quarter billion Facebook user profiles sold for around $540 on the dark web (or about 0.0002 cents per record).

Phew! That’s a lot — and yet that’s not even everything. Don’t forget that Facebook owns Instagram and WhatsApp, both of which have had their fair share of controversies, as well. Most other social networks have been rocked by scandals, too.

The “Privacy Paradox”

With the above in mind, it comes as no surprise that the majority of people are worried about their online privacy. In a recent study by the Dutch search engine company Startpage, 72% of Americans said they are either “extremely concerned” or “very concerned” about their privacy online.

Despite this, most people take no action to minimize their digital footprint, and almost 20% made no effort to protect their data in the last 12 months. Of those that did do something to protect their privacy, more than half merely updated a password.

  • Looking at social media in particular, only 57% of Facebook users have their accounts set to private, and 52% of Facebook users are willing to accept friend requests from strangers.
  • Things are not much better across other social media platforms: 42% of Instagram users allow anyone to view their Instagram stories, and only 19% of Twitter users keep their profile private. Moreover, only 55% of Twitter users have disabled locational data collection.

The growing discrepancy between user attitudes toward online privacy and their actual behavior is known as the “privacy paradox.”

How Is Your Social Networking Information Being Used — And By Whom

Cybercriminals can use social media profile data to take over accounts via credential stuffing or brute-forcing, craft successful social engineering scams, and commit identity theft.

Trolls may also use your social media information to doxx, harass, and stalk you. A 2020 study by the Pew Research Center found that about 4 in 10 Americans have been harassed online, with 75% of victims saying they were harassed on social media.

Employers frequently scan social media to determine job candidates’ suitability for a job. 9 in 10 employers look at prospective employees’ social media before hiring them, and almost 8 in 10 have denied a job to a candidate as a result of “inappropriate” social media content. Some employers may even fire workers for the things they say on social media.

Social media sites can share user data with third parties, like advertisers. Data brokers and people search sites may also crawl public social media profiles to compile detailed reports about individuals. Let’s also not forget Clearview AI, the controversial facial recognition company that scraped billions of images from social media to build an app that could identify pretty much anyone.

Law Enforcement officials have been monitoring social media activity ever since the days of MySpace. According to a 2016 report by the International Association of Chiefs of Police and Urban Institute, 70% of officers find social media a great tool for intelligence gathering. It was thanks to his Facebook status that the FBI was able to locate the fugitive Maxi Sopo. However, law enforcement may also use social media to identify protesters. Recently, the Department of Homeland Security was also sued for its obscure social media monitoring practices and the role these practices may have played in denying immigration attempts.

Ways to Protect Your Privacy on Social Media

Start with the basics and make your way down.

Basic:
  • Check the privacy settings of each social media account you have (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, LinkedIn).
  • Read the terms of service of each social media network that you use. Terms of Service Didn’t Read summarizes the terms of service for some of the most popular social media platforms (and other apps) for you.
  • Think of every social media post you share as public. A combination of private but still incriminating Facebook posts and a disloyal friend helped the police put the New York gang member Melvin Colon behind bars.
  • Don’t overshare. Information like your date of birth and hometown can give cybercriminals all the ammunition they need to steal your identity. Speaking of not oversharing, stay away from Facebook quizzes and be wary of posting photos/videos that may allow someone to guess your locations, relationships, etc.
  • Realize that everything you post on social media can stay on the internet forever. Anyone can take a screenshot of your post or use the Wayback Machine to search through deleted posts.
  • Don’t give unnecessary permissions. You can use MyPermissions Privacy Cleaner to see what information which app has (Android, iPhone).
  • Ask your family not to share photos, status updates, or posts about you.
  • Verify your connections, especially if you’re not sure if someone is actually who they say they are or if you have a feeling that you’ve already accepted that person as a friend.
  • Don’t sign in to third-party sites with social credentials and revoke unnecessary permissions to social apps you don’t use (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and LinkedIn).

Advanced:
  • Provide false information, like a pseudonym instead of your real name or a fake birthday.
  • Use a Masked Email address and phone number when signing up for a new account.
  • Remove metadata from your photos before posting them on social media.
  • Use tools like Fawkes to subtly change your photos to prevent facial recognition systems from being able to recognize your face. 
  • Consider alternatives to your favorite social media platforms. For example, MeWe is a potential alternative to Facebook.
Recommended Reads

Our recent favorites to keep you up to date in today’s digital privacy landscape.

Accellion Cyberattack Victims List Grows
In December 2020, the file-sharing software Accellion was hit by a zero-day exploit. While Accellion patched all known vulnerabilities immediately, hackers nonetheless had enough time to attack Accellion partners and customers. Current victims include New Zealand Central Bank, the energy trading company Shell, and numerous universities, among others.

Snap Tried to Circumvent Apple’s New Privacy Rules
Snap, the parent company of Snapchat, has researched ways to get around Apple’s new privacy rules that ban apps from collecting user data without their consent. The company reportedly looked at the possibility of gathering data from third parties and cross-referencing it against its own user database. However, Snap has since said it will not track individual users as it would break the rules but may gather data on user “cohorts.”

Job Hunters Targeted by a Spear-Phishing Campaign on LinkedIn
According to a recent report published by the security firm eSentire, a threat group known as Golden Chickens is distributing a fileless backdoor, more_eggs. After finding individuals that are looking for jobs, the group sends them a .zip file named after their current job role. Once the target opens the file, the fileless malware hijacks their device. The group is also selling the more_eggs backdoor as a service.

US Agencies Used Clearview AI’s Facial Recognition App Without Permission
Almost 2,000 US agencies — including local and state police and state healthcare organizations — used Clearview AI’s controversial app without permission. Between 2018 and 2020, more than 7,000 individuals from these organizations used the tool to locate petty criminals, Black Lives protesters, and their family and friends, running almost 340,000 searches in total. In most cases, management was unaware of what was going on.

You Asked, We Answered

Here are some of the questions our readers asked us last month.

Q: Is there anything I need to do before I delete my Facebook account? And will Facebook actually delete my data when I delete my account, or will they still keep it?

A: Good question! There are a few things you’ll likely want to do before you delete your Facebook account:
  1. Download a copy of all your data (i.e., old statuses, photos, friends, activity on Facebook marketplace, profile information, and more).
  2. Check where you use Facebook as your login details and add a second authentication option that isn’t Facebook. You don’t want to lose access to apps like Spotify or Amazon.
  3. Notify your friends you’re moving on and ask for an alternative means of contact.

To delete your Facebook account, you need to make sure you delete it, not deactivate it. If you deactivate your account, others won’t be able to see it, but you’ll still be able to return to it at a later date. On the other hand, if you delete your account, it’ll be gone for good.

As for whether Facebook will actually delete your data once you delete your account. Facebook says that “It may take up to 90 days from the beginning of the deletion process to delete all the things you've posted.”

However, “Copies of your information may remain after the 90 days in backup storage that we use to recover in the event of a disaster, software error, or other data loss event. We may also keep your information for things like legal issues, terms violations, or harm prevention efforts.”

Questioned on whether Facebook deletes all backups as well by Republican Senator Dean Heller during the 2018 Senate’s hearing, the CEO of Facebook Mark Zuckerberg said, “That is certainly the way it’s supposed to work.” Not exactly encouraging!

It’s worth bearing in mind that even if you delete your Facebook profile, the tech giant can still track you via “shadow accounts,” i.e., a hidden user profile compiled using information about you that you didn’t provide yourself.
