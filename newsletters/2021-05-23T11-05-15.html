Expats say Mexico is the happiest place on earth

What happened:

The Expat Insider survey asked over 12,000 expats of 174 different nationalities, living in 184 countries around the world, what they thought of their experience abroad.

The survey measured 37 factors related to quality of life, working abroad, personal finance, and the ease of settling in to a new location.

On the overall rankings, two frequent Sovereign Man Plan B destinations came in the top five— Mexico was second best, and Portugal fifth.

Mexico ranked:

  • Highest overall of any country on personal happiness scores— 89% of expats living in Mexico are happy with life in general.
  • First in ease of settling in— 85% say it’s easy to settle down, and 78% say it’s easy to make local friends.
  • Second for for leisure options.

Portugal ranked:

  • Third overall for quality of life— 84% report being happy with life in general.
  • Fourth for leisure options— 4 of 5 expats are happy with their own socializing opportunities.
  • In the top ten for safety and security— 97% report feeling safe.

What this means:

It’s a big world out there with a lot of opportunities.

But do you have access to all of those opportunities? And if so, can you count on always having that access?

For example, the strength of the US passport took a big hit during the pandemic. You still can’t use it to travel to the European Union (unless you’re vaccinated).

And Australia went totally nuts and returned to its roots as a prison colony. The Prime Minister claims it is still not safe to allow even vaccinated Australians to leave the country.

This would not be a great time to rely solely on an Australian passport for travel freedom.

But by obtaining residency in a foreign country, you can make sure you always have at least one other place to live, work, study, invest, and raise a family.

And if you take it one step further and obtain foreign citizenship, you assure that one government is not in total control of your ability to travel.

What you can do about it:

Both Mexico and Portugal allow foreigners to easily obtain residency. And both offer a path to full citizenship and a second passport.

Portugal’s passport is especially sought after, because it allows you to live and work in any country in the European Union.

Mexico became an extremely desirable location after remaining relatively free and unrestricted throughout the pandemic.

Here is a quick overview of how to obtain residency and citizenship in each.

Mexico

You can obtain temporary residency in Mexico by making an appointment at your local embassy to submit your residency application, then traveling to Mexico to complete the application and pick up your residency card. To qualify you must have a monthly income of about $2,150 or savings of about $40,000 (it varies at different embassies).

This allows you to stay in Mexico indefinitely.

You must renew the card after one year— but once you renew it, it is good for another three years.

At that point, after four years total as a temporary resident, you can apply for permanent residency. That means never having to renew again— it is truly permanent.

If you’re already retired, and have about $3,500 per month in retirement income or $100,000 in savings, you can skip right to obtaining permanent residency.

And after five years of temporary or permanent residency, you become eligible to apply for full citizenship, which comes with a Mexican passport. You’ll have to live in Mexico for at least 18 months of the previous two years before applying for citizenship.

Portugal

With the D7 visa you can stay in Portugal for two years before you have to renew it. To qualify, you must have an income of about €665 per month, or €15,960 in savings. This is the bare minimum, its always better to show more.

The D7 visa comes with minimum stay requirements. You must stay in Portugal at least four months per year in total, AND you cannot be away from Portugal for longer than six months in a row. That means you will need to spend at least 6 months if you go to Portugal once, and as little as 4 months in total if you are willing to go there twice a year or more.

Another option is to gain residency using Portugal’s Golden Visa program, which only requires you be present in the country for one week each year on average.

But the Golden Visa requires an investment of between €280,000 and €500,000— depending on the type of investment or property you wish to make.

With either type of residency, you become eligible to apply for citizenship after five years. And yes, going the Golden Visa route means you could become eligible for citizenship after spending an average of just one week per year in the country for the past five years.
