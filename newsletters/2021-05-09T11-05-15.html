Government Searches for Loopholes to Spy on You

What happened:

The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) is looking into loopholes it can exploit to “legally” spy on Americans.

The plan is to hire government contractors to infiltrate private social media groups, and encrypted private group chats. That way, so the theory goes, the DHS won’t be violating the Fourth Amendment right against unreasonable search.

This is similar to a tactic which Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) used last year to collect location data on thousands of people without a warrant. ICE simply bought the data from private companies.

Both the DHS and ICE claim they are targeting aggregate data and trends, not individuals. But the capability exists, in both cases, to track individuals without a warrant.

It’s unclear, however, why the DHS doesn’t just ask the Post Office to spy for them.

In April, it was revealed that the Post Office was spying on Americans’ social media posts.

A leaked bulletin to other federal policing agencies stated:

“Analysts with the United States Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) Internet Covert Operations Program (iCOP) monitored significant activity regarding planned protests occurring internationally and domestically on March 20, 2021... iCOP analysts are currently monitoring these social media channels for any potential threats stemming from the scheduled protests and will disseminate intelligence updates as needed.”

What this means:

The government is supposed to have a warrant, targeted at a specific person or space, based on probable cause, to investigate and collect information.

They clearly already ignore these rules, but still try to keep the thinnest veneer of legal justification as plausible deniability.

But long before these recent developments, documents leaked by Edward Snowden showed that the US and its foreign allies already spy on each others’ citizens, in order to ignore the rule against domestic surveillance of their own citizens without a warrant.

What you can do about it:

Clearly it would be an uphill battle to attempt to bolster your privacy by reforming various US government agencies.

The only solution is to take personal control of your privacy.

Trying to protect yourself from government spy agencies might sound daunting.

But a few basic data privacy best practices will make a huge difference, and protect you from cyber-criminals and as well as hackers.

First understand where the threat is coming from to decide which tools you need to deploy.

We’re talking about three different threats in this article:

  1. The DHS and other government agencies infiltrating and spying on private social media groups
  2. Bulk location data sold to ICE and other government agencies
  3. Intelligence agencies surveilling and collecting internet communications

The first threat may not require a technical solution.

It doesn’t matter how strong the encryption on a Signal group chat is if a member of the group is a government or media informant.

The solution might be to strive for anonymity, not privacy.

For example, whatever account you want to remain anonymous on should not connect back to any personally identifying information. Obviously if you join that rebellious Facebook group with your real profile, this won’t work.

It requires compartmentalizing— dividing certain aspects of your digital life so that a breach in one does not carry over to others.

You could do this by generating new phone numbers (from voice over IP providers) and email addresses (for example, using ProtonMail) for each communication app that requires one.

Open a new browser (ideally something privacy focused like Brave) each time you log in, to avoid cross tab tracking.

All the while, use a VPN (Virtual Private Network).

A VPN creates a secure, encrypted tunnel from your device to the VPN provider’s server. Instead of connecting to your ISP (Internet Service Provider) and then to the Internet, a VPN allows you to connect, via an encrypted tunnel, through your ISP to the VPN provider.

No one in between you and the VPN provider can see what’s happening inside the tunnel, so if someone is listening in on your local WiFi, or if the government is gobbling up all traffic going through your ISP, you’re protected.

Once your traffic leaves the VPN provider’s servers, no one knows it is your internet traffic— assuming that the VPN does not keep logs on its users, and assuming you don’t give out personally identifying information.

The second threat is a matter of deciding how much personal data you are willing to trade for convenience.

Google maps is very convenient. But do you want Google, and whoever it decides to share with/ sell to, knowing everywhere you go?

If at some point the US government wants that information, as was the case with ICE, it’s in their reach.

But there are alternatives to Google and Apple maps which allow you to download maps to navigate offline, while still providing turn by turn directions.

As examples (not recommendations) MagicEarth and OsmAnd each allow you to download maps for offline navigation to enhance your privacy.

But it’s worth keeping in mind that other apps can also track your location. The fewer apps on your phone, the fewer opportunities for spies to gather information on you.

The third threat can be solved with strong encryption.

Encrypted messaging works if both sides are using the same service.

Using encrypted email services like ProtonMail, and communicating with other ProtonMail users means no one can intercept the conversation. But communicating with a Gmail user means there is no privacy, because Gmail is not encrypted (and scans messages for advertising purposes).

Same goes for encrypted messaging apps— both users must be using encryption to prevent the interception of messages.

ProtonMail does offer a product in its paid services where you can send encrypted messages to users who do not use encrypted email.

But the surest way to communicate without anyone, including the government, being able to read your communications, is to use end to end encrypted services on both sides of the communication.
