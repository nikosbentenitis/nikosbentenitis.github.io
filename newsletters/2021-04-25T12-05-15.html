April 25, 2021

Why does a single deli have a $100 million stock valuation?

What happened:

Your Hometown Deli in Paulsboro, New Jersey was closed from March 23 to September 8 of last year due to Covid-19. That’s probably why it registered only $35,748 in sales in the last two years combined.

Less clear is why the deli’s parent company, Hometown International, has a valuation of $100 MILLION.

Your Hometown Deli is the single location of Hometown International, a publicly traded company worth $12.86 per share, at the time of this writing. It’s an over the counter (OTC) or “pink sheet” stock, which requires less disclosure and fewer compliance rules from the SEC.

The stock rarely trades more than a few hundred shares per day.

The local Paulsboro High School wrestling coach and principal is the President and CEO.

The Chairman of the Board’s father is paid $15,000 per month in consulting fees from the company, which posted a $624,438 loss for 2020.

While the deli was closed during lockdowns last year, the stock price went from $3.25 per share, all the way up to a 52-week high of $15.75 last week.

As the hedge fund manager who brought this to the media’s attention remarked, “The pastrami must be amazing.”

What this means:

Clearly whatever is truly happening with this deli is not Kosher.

But there are almost certainly people exposed to this stock who are not in on it.

And then there are countless stocks which aren’t as blatantly a scam, but are basically less egregious versions of Hometown International.

The price to earnings (P/E) of Hometown International is about 2,850.

But even a mainstream stock, with actual products, some profits, and a quirky CEO trades at a P/E of about 1,180— Tesla.

The historic average P/E for stocks is closer to 15— as in, if you bought 100% of the company’s shares, it would take 15 years (without reinvesting earnings) to recoup your initial investment. Not a millennium (Tesla)... or two... or nearly three (Hometown).

As Simon wrote last week in a Notes From the Field article on inflation:

“Stocks are trading at peak valuations; the average Price/Earnings ratio in the S&P 500, for example, is now 42, roughly 3x the historic average. It has only been higher two other times– just before the 2000 crash, and just before the 2008 crash.”

Hometown International is the type of business you see pop up during bubbles. Everything is making money in the stock market— even a high school wrestling coach’s deli.

What you can do about it:

There are still ways to invest during these crazy time.

Value investing— some stocks are still worth buying.

Members of Sovereign Man’s investment newsletter The 4th Pillar will be aware of what value investing is.

Sovereign Man’s Chief Investment Analyst Tim Staermose manages to find publicly listed companies trading at a valuation lower than the amount of cash on hand.

For example, a company with $100 million of cash on hand, no debt, and a market capitalization of $95 million would have a 5% discount to net cash.

That means that if you were capable of buying every share in the company to own it outright, you could take the cash, shut down the company, and still make a profit.

Of course that’s not the actual plan. It just demonstrates how absurd valuations can be in financial markets.

Value investing is taking advantage of the mismatch between a company’s stock price and its actual value.

If the company also has strong financials and good leadership, chances are that the stock price will rise to a more realistic valuation when the market catches on.

This is essentially the exact opposite of what is happening with many popular US stocks.

Many of the stocks recommended in The 4th Pillar are companies listed on exchanges throughout Asia, which has the benefit of diversifying outside of US dollars and assets.

But if you don’t want the risk of stocks, and simply want to protect your savings against inflation...

Precious metals like gold and silver are still an obvious insurance policy.

That is especially true given that they have been largely left out of the “everything bubble.”

While real estate, stocks, and bond prices have continued to balloon, gold and silver saw an initial surge towards the beginning of the pandemic, and then settled down.

But while fiat currencies like the USD may continue to decline in value, 5,000 years of history suggests the value of gold and silver will remain relatively constant. (Specifically, the value of precious metals as expressed in fiat currencies should rise. Meanwhile, your gold or silver’s future purchasing or exchange power - what really matters - will remain.)

You can own precious metals outright and keep them in a home safe, meaning there is no counter-party risk.

Or you can trust a secure storage facility in a safe jurisdiction. That comes with some counter party risk. But if you are concerned about capital controls, or gold nationalization, as happened under President Franklin D. Roosevelt, this is an option to consider.

You can send Silver Bullion your existing gold and silver, or precious metals through their platform and then store it there.

If you choose this protection, and your home country attempts to nationalize gold, “you will be unable to have the assets sent to the nationalising country even if required to do so under your jurisdiction’s law. The assets would safely remain in Singapore until you can then take possession of your holdings personally at a later time.”

That’s an extra level of protection against your home government.

There are plenty of other real assets which hold value.

Certain art, wine, watches, antique weapons, and so many other collectibles can also hold or increase in value during inflationary times.

If there is scarcity and desirability, chances are it fits the bill. Generally it makes sense to invest in something you’re interested in, as it puts you in a more informed position to buy— or at least enjoy the research. Plus you get some enjoyment out of owning it.

For example, for a history buff, a sword from the American Civil War might make sense, because it is scarce— no authentic new Civil War era swords can be produced— and it is desirable— enough Civil War history enthusiasts exist to form a market demand for the sword.

However you choose to safeguard or invest your savings, just be aware of the many stock valuations out there that are full of baloney.
