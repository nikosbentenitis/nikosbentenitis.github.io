​Standardized Tests and the SAT​

If there’s something I’ve changed my mind on in the past year, it’s the value of the SAT. I hated taking the SAT in high school. Besides, I spent tons of time on test prep that was neither fun nor successful. I doubled down on my thinking once I started having career success as an adult, which made me say: “See, the SAT doesn’t work!”

I can’t believe I’m saying this, but now that I’ve looked into it, I’m supportive of standardized tests. No, they’re not fun. Yes, they don’t measure every kind of intelligence. But the data shows that they do exactly what they strive to do: level the playing field so that everybody can demonstrate their competence, no matter who they are or where they come from.

These three lines from a recent study stick out:

  1. “The predictive power of the SAT holds even when researchers control for socioeconomic status."
    ​
  2. "It is remarkably difficult to increase an individual’s SAT score."
    ​
  3. "High SAT scores are generally difficult to acquire by any means other than high ability.”

​
Measurement will never be perfect; I’ve written about that before. Neither will it ever be a perfect measure of intelligence, and definitely other personality traits like creativity or persistence. But it seems like the SAT is pretty good at achieving what it’s trying to do.

​How Bill Gates Thinks​

In 1993, the founder of Oracle, Larry Ellison, spoke on the phone with Bill Gates. He called it the most interesting conversation he’s ever had with Bill.

Ellison wrote: "It was around eleven o'clock in the morning, and we were on the phone discussing some technical issue, I don't remember what it was. Anyway, I didn't agree with him on some point, and I explained my reasoning. Bill says, 'I'll have to think about that, I'll call you back."

Four hours later, Bill Gates called back and said: "Yeah, I think you're right about that, but what about A and B and C?' I said, 'Bill, have you been thinking about this for the last five hours?"

Ellison recounted: "I was just stunned. He had taken the time and effort to think it all through and had decided I was right and he was wrong. Now, most people hate to admit they're wrong, but it didn't bother Bill one bit. All he cared about was what was right, not who was right.”

Photo of the Day

One of the biggest differences between the East Coast and the West Coast is how much people respect history. Ever since the Gold Rush, the West Coast has been about rejecting the past to build a better future. First, for yourself. Now, for yourself and the world (in theory).

But that attitude has a blind spot. Anecdotally, it seems like people in Hollywood and Silicon Valley don’t know the history of their industries as well as East Coasters. One reason why is that West Coasters are more likely to be transplants. The number of people you meet in New York who grew up there is much higher than in San Francisco. Since industries have historically been geographically concentrated, you can’t know the history of an industry without knowing the history of the place it grew up. Cars were concentrated in Detroit, software was concentrated in Silicon Valley, and film was concentrated in Los Angeles.

Here’s an anecdote: You’re probably familiar with the celebrity gossip magazine TMZ. The name refers to the “thirty mile zone” in Los Angeles. There are specific laws for film work done within the circle. For example, any piece of entertainment produced within the zone is considered local. Workers therefore have to pay for their own meals and transportation inside the zone, which reduces labor costs.

Knowing the history of your industry better than anybody else is a huge career advantage, which is wild because even though it's so simple, few people read up on it.
