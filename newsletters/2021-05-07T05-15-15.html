The Maginot Line Problem

The Maginot Line was a line of concrete fortifications, obstacles and weapons built by France in the 1930s to deter invasion by Germany.

When it was built, the Maginot Line was believed to be one of the most significant innovations in national defense. Foreign leaders came from all over to tour it.

It was an impressive-looking series of fortresses and railroads, resistant to all known forms of artillery. It had underground railways that made it invulnerable to bombings and tank fire and state-of-the-art living conditions with air conditioning for stationed troops.

French military experts extolled the Line as a work of genius that would deter German aggression. They believed that it would both deter Germany and, in the case of an attack, it could slow an invasion force long enough for French forces to mobilise and counterattack.

The Maginot Line extended into the border with Belgium but stopped at the Forest of Ardennes because of the prevailing belief among experts that Ardennes was impenetrable.

To spoil the ending, the Ardennes was not impenetrable.

At the start of the war, The Germans deployed a small force to the Maginot Line as a decoy then sent a million men and 1500 tanks through the "impenetrable" Ardennes.

A rapid advance through the forest encircled much of the Allied forces, resulting in a sizeable force being evacuated at Dunkirk leaving the forces to the south unable to mount an effective resistance to the German invasion of France.

The Germans attacked on May 10th and by June 14th, Partis fell to the German army. On June 22, the French government signed an armistice. A feat that had not been accomplished in five years of bloody trench warfare happened in just over five weeks.

This is not a phenomenon isolated to War, but broadly applicable to any complex system: be it a company, investment strategy, or organism.

Why did this happen? Why were the French so wrong and defeated so quickly?

Paradigm Research

As you may have noticed, or as your random cousin may have texted 14 times this year, the price of cryptocurrencies has gone up and interest in them alongside it. I’ve written previously about how public blockchains like Bitcoin and Ethereum work and why I believe they are valuable and interesting.

One area of the space which I have been trying to understand better is automated market makers, the most popular of which is known as Uniswap.

In effect, automated market makers are a smart contract protocol that allows users to trade between different crypto assets without relying on a centralized exchange such as Coinbase or Binance.

I think the emergent properties of this sort of system are really interesting long-term and this is a nice introduction to what they are and how they work.

Moses Kagan - Permanent Real Estate Investing
The Business Brew

I mentioned previously that I’ve been trying to get my chops up on residential real estate. One person who I’ve found helpful in that quest is Moses Kagan. This was an interview he did with Bill Brewster talking about his experience investing in multifamily residential real estate in Los Angeles.

Real estate was not an institutional asset class until I think like the late 90s. So the people who before that, it was just a bunch of like weird families and there wasn't the internet and books and bigger pockets and all these things that transmit real estate knowledge, normal people. Now, it was like a weird niche skill set that people kind of developed on the side and the buildings were cheaper and the returns were much higher because there was much less capital floating around in the asset class. So it's just like the biggest private landowner in Los Angeles is a korean dentist. He just started syndicating buildings after the Rodney King riots and just kept going.

It made me think that maybe part of the reason real estate was attractive in the Rich Dad, Poor Dad era (~1980s) is because it was an illegible and inefficient market and now it has become much more legible.

That’s not to say it’s not still a good asset class, but that perhaps a good deal of the "juice" over the last forty years has been squeezed out.

He also shared what I thought was an insightful summary of how they think about investing in real estate.

We're totally driven by unlevered yield on cost. And it's just an incredibly simple calculation. It is basically a cap rate, for everyone who doesn't know what that is, you take your your annual rents and you subtract your annual operating expenses, not including a mortgage. So just like your property taxes and trash pickup and lawn and all that stuff. Okay. And that resulting number rents minus annual expenses is your net operating income? Okay. That's you're just the unlevered cash flow that's coming out of the building. [To get your cap rate], you just divided by the purchase price.

That number will be like 5% of its a five cap or it'll be 7% if it's a seven cap. And the concept is that if you invest a million dollars, you'll in a seven cap building, you ought to get with no mortgage. You ought to get $70,000 a year in that operating income for us.

The equation is very similar except that we're not just buying the building, we are also renovating it. So it's forecast net operating income on the top and the numerator and the denominator is the cost of buying the building and the cost of fixing it up.
That's far and away the thing that we focus on most when we're looking at deals.

One of my frustrations (annoyances?) with real estate books is that many of them make the implicit assumption that prices always go up (my other frustration is they don’t factor in the value of your time).

And, like, it’s really not very hard to invest in something that always goes up so if that were the case then there really wouldn’t be any need to read a real estate book, you would just lever yourself up to your eyeballs and buy as much as possible. Indeed, some people do this and it is basically always a bad idea- nothing goes up forever (though it can go up for much longer than anyone expects - the market can stay irrational longer than you can remain solvent and all that).

Thinking about it in terms of unlevered yield and cash flow is a much more robust model in my opinion as rents tend to be a bit stickier than prices and if you’re doing it, then that seems like a reasonable way to manage your risk. Even if rents fall 30%, on an unlevered basis and your 7% yield goes down to 4.9% which is not ideal, but likely still very manageable.
