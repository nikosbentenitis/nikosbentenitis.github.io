Coolest Things I Learned This Week

Writing: Use Surprising Analogies

If you want to write consistently and consciously develop your style, you need a series of heuristics you can depend on.

One of my favorites is: “Use Surprising Analogies.”

The idea came to me in a conversation a couple of years ago. We were talking about how our best conversations go in wild directions. We know we’re onto something when we make analogies that have never been made before. That’s a good heuristic for writing too — as long as they’re also easy to understand.

That’s why I say that we’re living in “A Microwave Economy” and why I compare Kansas City BBQ to email marketing in this video. Cliches are the antithesis of good writing, and surprising analogies are a fun way to make your writing fresh.

Netflix Should Buy Masterclass

Netflix should buy Masterclass and double down on education.

Like Masterclass, their shows should be fun, fast-paced, and built on celebrity talent. Like everything that works well on Netflix, the content should stay relevant over time. To make sure that happens, the education initiative should focus on slow-to-change topics like math, chemistry, and economics.

Unbundling Childhood Education

I spent the weekend talking about the future of childhood education. Nobody is happy with the current system. Students are bored, parents are disappointed with what their kids are learning, and teachers are worked like cattle on a hot summer day. Until now, the Internet hasn’t really touched childhood education. Kids are still going to school every day to learn from teachers who deliver information.

The Internet is going to change that. Right now, teachers are a bundle of two roles: childcare and information delivery. But the Internet will turn those into different roles. The Internet has already made learning cheap while improving the quality of information delivery.

Childcare is expensive though. My friends who’ve done Teach for America barely spend any time teaching. Away from the classroom, they’re bogged down in bureaucratic nonsense. Once they step inside the classroom, many of their students have tragic family circumstances and little access to healthy food. Thus, the bottlenecks in education have more to do with childcare than learning.

I’d like to see a school experiment with a new kind of teacher. One who is explicitly focused on childcare. When it comes to learning, they should be familiar with the educational options on the Internet. Stuff like Synthesis, Primer, and videos on YouTube. Then, they can send different students to different programs based on their temperament and individual struggles. In turn, they’ll serve less like teachers and more like facilitators who rely on Internet resources to do the information delivery.

The Second Order Effects of Crypto Wealth

By one rough estimate, half of the world’s billionaires will be crypto billionaires when the price of Bitcoin reaches $200,000.

They’ll be a whole new kind of billionaire. They’ll be new money billionaires who are disproportionately young and technologically literate. More importantly, they will have built their wealth by betting against "the system.”

Philanthropy dollars will be reallocated as a result, which Tyler Cowen explained here: "They are unlikely to fund the ongoing labor costs of established cultural institutions. Bitcoin and many other cryptocurrencies seem designed to stand independent of any government or mainstream financial institution. That too suggests that the philanthropic emphasis of crypto wealth will be on non-establishment, non-governmental organizations.”

It’s not just the technology that’s going to be transformative. It’s the way that people who become wealthy because of crypto will choose to allocate their dollars too.
