May 16, 2021

“Thin privilege” is yet another way to target “oppressors”

What happened:

If you’re white, male, and straight, you probably already knew you were privileged. But have you ever considered if you have thin privilege?

You see, overweight people are victims of discrimination due to unfair stigmas associating obesity with laziness and lack of self control.

But don’t worry, the woke warriors of social justice are here to set society straight.

In April, Good Housekeeping ran an article called, “What Is Thin Privilege? And why we need to talk about it, according to fat activists and their allies.”

It’s part of a series of articles Good Housekeeping is running throughout 2021 on challenging “the messages we are given about what makes us attractive, successful and healthy.”

“Thin privilege is something that those of power and influence in Western society have collectively chosen over time.”

The article explains that “people tend to see a person with flat abs and automatically think they are ‘disciplined’ — or as a person who takes care of themselves. Positive judgements like these, true or not, can make it easier for a person to get a job, get more pay, make friends, network, date, and so on. (You can guess what being seen as “lazy,” “weak-willed,” or “careless" for having a belly gets you.)”

And of course, “Thin privilege — like other types of privilege — is intersectional.”

“Black women face some of the most criticism and discrimination because of how racism, sexism, and fatphobia often go hand in hand.”

Sonalee Rashatwar, a “fat South Asian nonbinary” therapist from Philadelphia, has the solution.

Rashatwar counsels people against intentional weight loss, instead explaining “how sizeism is affected by racism, misogyny, classism, homophobia, transphobia, and ableism.”

“They-he” says the solution to “white supremacists fatphobia” is to undo Western civilization. Last fall, Rashatwar even participated in a panel discussion which included a segment on how to “unlearn internalized capitalism,” (which cost $10 to attend).

What this means:

Intersectionality is how the modern woke Bolsheviks divide society into rival groups, similar to how classic Marxists pitted the working class proletariat against the property-owning bourgeoisie.

Intersectionality collects all the “victim” groups together to oppose the “privileged” or “oppressor” groups.

Victims include fat, black, brown, female, trans, gay, crippled, and Muslim people.

Oppressors include thin, white, male, straight, able-bodied, and Christian people.

Being a victim gives you special rights, privileges, and moral superiority. Being privileged means you are okay to hate—and punish—for propping up an oppressive system.

“Cancel culture” is a manifestation of self-proclaimed victims punishing oppressors for perceived aggression or “micro-aggression”— prejudice almost too subtle to notice. They equate words with violence, which is why free speech is not kosher in the woke religion.

The idea is to shut down debate and force people to self-censor for fear of repercussions.

The end result is that large swaths of society become afraid to say anything substantive out of fear of unwittingly offending the wrong woke mobster.

And that’s how radical, dangerous, violent ideas become mainstream. Respect for free speech, even offensive speech, has the opposite effect, because open debate trounces bad ideas.

We’re in a social phase where challenging the woke intersectionality mindset can invite shaming, rage, ridicule, loss of material well-being, and violence.

What you can do about it:

A Plan B is typically about giving yourself options to protect against the negative repercussions of governments’ laws, taxes, regulations, and monetary policy.

But in the current “cancel culture” environment, it makes sense to also think about the social aspects of your Plan B.

Clearly the woke mob is out to get anyone who is successful and productive— hence the focus on dismantling Western civilization and destroying capitalism.

If you somehow land in their cross-hairs, internet activists may take something you said out of context, or dig up “offensive” posts, videos, or rumors from years earlier, perhaps even when you were a teenager or a child.

It makes sense to take a look over your Plan B, and see if you are robust or even anti-fragile against these types of social dangers.

Robust means you would survive an attack. Antifragile means you would actually “gain from disorder,” as Nassim Nicholas Taleb puts it in his book, Antifragile.

For example, Jordan Peterson only gained fame and fortune after student mobs tried to cancel the professor. He opposed a Canadian law that would have made it a crime to not use a person’s preferred pronouns.

That’s an example of anti-fragility.

Some questions to ask yourself:

Is your income dependent on your good public standing?

For example, teachers or Disney employees could be out of a job if they dare speak out against Critical Race Theory invading schools and offices.

Something to focus on could be finding sources of passive, investment, or side income. Just committing fifteen minutes per day to learning about investment strategy could make a huge cumulative difference.

Does your business rely on service providers ready or willing to cave to a woke mob?

We all saw how after the January 6 capitol riots, anyone who questioned the election results became a target.

Some businesses and individuals lost access to website hosting servers, payment processors, and social media.

You might want to review the policies and past behavior of some of the companies you rely on. Understand the consequences of losing access to them, and line up backups and alternatives.

Are you vulnerable to being “doxxed”— where activists share your personal information online, like phone numbers or addresses, to bring threats and intimidation?

There are many strategies to increase online privacy. You could even go so far as to buy a home under a trust to shield your identity from being attached to the location.

But most of us just need to follow basic personal data best practices like guarding our real phone number, and compartmentalizing our data so that one breach or leak doesn’t grant access to all of our online accounts.

A Plan B is all about giving yourself piece of mind by preparing for the worst. That way if you ever become the victim of cancel culture, it won’t ruin your life.
